Behavior Tree

In developing this game, my primary focus was enhancing enemy behaviors by implementing a Behavior Tree (BT) system. This experience marked my first encounter with dynamic AI design, as my prior experience had been limited to static behaviors in games like One Last Quest.

At the project's start, I researched AI systems in gaming. While exploring various methodologies, I intentionally decided against Goal-Oriented Action Planning (GOAP) due to its complexities, which were unnecessary for this game. Consequently, I narrowed my options to two prominent frameworks: Behavior Trees and Finite State Machines. Ultimately, I used a Behavior Tree since I deemed it more relevant after talking to my professor.

One of the significant challenges I encountered was creating a robust infrastructure for the Behavior Tree. My initial research prompted two prevalent approaches: storage-based and tick-based. I initially experimented with the tick-based approach, but due to inexperience, the system needed to be fixed, and I needed to rewrite it. At this point, I pivoted to a more custom solution, leveraging an event-driven interrupt system.

This architecture allows for dynamic interruptions within the BT triggered by specific in-game events. While initially considering a steady reevaluation of the tree after each node completion, this approach proved clunky, specifically with decorator nodes such as repeaters. As a result, I developed an event-driven mechanism in which each node subscribes to its child upon activation, which is called after the child is finished.

Design Direction

In our initial concept pitch, we envisioned a game in which players embody the guardian of the forest. Central to this concept was a symbiotic relationship with the spirit tree, which would serve the player with a secondary empowerment system rooted in emotions. Each emotion, such as fear or happiness, would give the player specific boons. For instance, fear might give a protective shield, while happiness could provide passive health regeneration.

Our vision included a dynamic system where the tree would learn and adapt to the player's needs over time. If the player took damage, the tree might shift to an emotion like fear or, during a calmer moment, to hapiness to restore the player's health.

Additionally, we intended for each emotional state to be linked to a unique ultimate ability. While players could freely customize their four basic abilities between unlocked ones, the ultimate attack would always align with the forest's emotional state. For example, if the forest is in surprise, players might command trees to strike enemies, while sadness could create a flood to push back enemies and slow them.

However, during development, the game's scope had to be significantly decreased due to various challenges, including conflicting project schedules and a team member's departure. Unfortunately, this led to a big cutdown where these systems were included. In hindsight, I recognize the need for earlier scoping adjustments. Still, these realizations came too late in the development process, resulting in a simplified final product without these mechanics.
